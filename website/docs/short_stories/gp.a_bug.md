---
title: A Bug
---

The heat trapped in the black tar for the entire day was finally being released,
and I was coming back from work yet again after midnight. It was a hot night in
the middle of August. I squeezed through the iron bars of the pedestrian gate
to get to the shortcut entrance in the wall, the thick walls like a citadel.
The bars were so close they made it difficult for people to come through.

I quickly walked the distance between the shortcut through the wall and the
anodized aluminum door of the building. The door was worn from repeated
slamming: the glass, the metal vertical stripes protecting it, the knob—all
loose. It was a dark yellow color. It was absurd that someone made cheap doors
and painted them with a color resembling gold. Swiftly I went up the 4 floors
of stairs and got as quietly as possible in our apartment.

Right after the door clicked closed, while heading towards our bedroom, I
stepped on something: I felt it under my shoe and managed to turn my foot
slightly (my senses heightened since I didn't want to wake her), but the
creature easily gave up and crunched under my weight. It was a cockroach, the
size of a large coin, now a drop of black ink on the white glazed tile. Shit, I
would have to clean the mess in the morning.

I slipped into bed and started trying to find the right position to fall asleep,
crawling down to have the pillow in the nook of my neck and settling into my
spot. After a few minutes, she woke and said with a voice thick with sleep:

"I am afraid."
"Afraid of what?"
"Afraid to die ... I was swimming in the black river and suddenly a whirlpool
started pulling my left leg down ... until I went all under water and started
to drown ..."
"Everything is ok, my love. It was only a bad dream. Go back to sleep.
Everything is ok now."
"... I was breathing water, going down towards the black bottom, and trying to
swim up towards the light but ..."
"Everything is ok now. Go back to sleep. It was just a nightmare."
"Okay," she said yawning, her voice becoming slow with sleep again.

I lay flat on my back and she curled close to me. The windows were open because
of the heat. Sounds from the summer night drifted in through the gentle
movements of air oscillating in the rising heat. My mind registered the various
sounds as if switching channels.

Many cars sped on the nearby highway under the orange glow of sodium lights,
their tires making a continuous low sound, like water streaming through dams of
rocks and wet branches. A woman cried in the apartment on the other side of the
wall, then shouted; a deep male voice apologized, then grew angry, growing
louder and louder, the woman cursing, the sound of slammed doors and objects
(plates?) breaking on the floor.

A baby's cry, muffled from upstairs; a light switched on, a long "shh" sound
trying to quiet the screaming creature. Then, somewhere far away, dogs barking
furiously—a few days earlier, coming back later than usual, I had seen people
tie dogs to nearby light poles, beat them, and incite them to fight each other.

Other people were turning under the sheets, weighed down by their problems: how
to find the money to pay back, will my disease return?... I was also restless
with so many thoughts crowding my mind, the usual thoughts that awaken at night,
haunting sleep.

She was still moving under the thin white sheet, shaking and twitching in her
light sleep. She woke again asking what time it was. I answered: two.

The yellow door downstairs opened and closed. Someone paced nervously back and
forth in front of it. More people entered and exited the building, walking the
stairs, searching for their keys, scuffing shoe soles on the stairs, someone
missing a step.

It was a busy night in the building. Were summer weekday nights always so busy?
How had I never noticed before? Or was it just an extraordinary night? Yet
another thought emerged from the emptiness, filling the restless confusion of my
mind.

I still could not find peace. Maybe I needed some fresh water. I carefully
slipped out from the sheets, shifting my body slowly out of bed. I slipped into
the pair of sandals lying on the floor and went to the bathroom. When I turned
on the bathroom light, I saw again the black drop of ink on the floor near the
door. It was moving. The border of the drop was pulsating, like a drop of heavy
mercury trying to break the surface separating it from the outside world. What
the hell...? Maybe I was just dreaming; maybe the exhaustion from lack of sleep
was making me see things that weren't there... but it moved again. A slight
vibration. I was awake, and the drop was definitely moving. I moved closer and
squatted down to look. The cockroach's shell was flattened, already dissolving
into the black mass. But one leg of what used to be a cockroach, the third on
the left, to be precise, was still moving, paddling as if trying to swim
upstream, escaping the current pulling it down—the same black current she had
dreamed of, pulling her under. The little black stick had been moving against
the current for almost two hours. How much longer would it keep trying to escape
the inevitable? It must have been exhausted from the immense waste of energy.
A grim life in filth, and then a painful death. With my sandals, I smashed it
again, leaving a gray smear on the tiles.

I went back to bed. She was sleeping now with a relaxed expression on her face,
her breathing slow and even, no longer drowning. Everything was quiet outside:
no doors slamming, no more dogs barking, just the air moving in swirls from the
heat. Then I fell asleep.
