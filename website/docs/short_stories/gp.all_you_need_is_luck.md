---
title: All You Need Is Luck
---

The laboratory was built below the river because Giovanni Perri believed water
muffled coincidence. He liked that thought: above them the city gambled with
weather, traffic lights, ambition; below, computers and cables hummed with
obedience bordering on faith. The air was cool, static-charged, humming with the
low frequency of server farms. Monitors cast blue light across the glass
observation chamber, the chamber where he spent most of the days.

The machine reconstructed the past with perfect clarity, annotating every
outcome, exploring all the possible hidden branches: promotions that depended on
an elevator delay, marriages determined by a dropped glove, careers ended by a
missed bus.

When missing variables were assumed, more were added and the branches in the
past multiplied.

Perri tested athletes, generals, thieves, and prodigies. A sprinter who broke a
world record because the rival in the next lane had stomachache that morning .
In every case the decisive factor arrived earlier than effort: the birth hour, a
teacher's migraine, a coin tossed by a distracted cashier.

The machine measured competence by simulating all possible events in every
possible branch. The results were always surprising and unstable. Nothing seemed
to survive a small perturbation.

The city above continued rewarding success and merit. Ceremonies, speeches,
praises to crown discipline and hard work. The machine calmly highlighted the
rainstorm that had kept stronger candidates home, the pothole that twisted a
rival's ankle, the misaddressed email that cancelled an important meeting.

"Success is a story we tell after probability stabilizes," Perri annotated in
his lab book, his handwriting tightening as the weeks wore on.

For three nights Perri slept in the glass chamber. He watched his own career
unfold across simulations: every paper he had written emerged not from
brilliance but from being bored on a train that once stalled outside Naples.
Remove the stall, results changed, and the world-renowned Perri became somebody
who failed to achieve tenure or start the subterranean laboratory.

Perri always believed his supervisor chose him over a stronger candidate out of
genuine recognition. He ran the simulation. The machine said the stronger
candidate was offered the position first and declined due to a family illness.

On the fourth night, he simulated the night he met his wife in a bar. The
machine showed three other women he spoke to that evening. In branch 7491, he
married the woman in the blue dress—they divorced after six years. In branch
7829, he married the redhead—two children, a good life, though he drank heavily.
The machine ranked all iterations. The one he married was statistically the
happiest choice, but not by much. The margins were small. Probabilistic noise.

He sat with that result for hours. The blue light from the monitors caught her
in his thoughts—the specific way she smiled, which he had believed was chosen by
him, which he had believed meant something. The machine said it was a cascade of
accidents: his friend's cancelled dinner, a coat check attendant forgetting her
umbrella, a bartender pouring slowly on a specific drink.

He reached for the keyboard to run another branch. His hand stopped. He did not
run it again.

On the fifth night, he ran a control experiment: himself. He input his own
current state—this moment, sleeping in the glass chamber—and asked: what is the
next significant choice I make?

The simulation flickered. Then stabilized: 14:47 tomorrow, Giovanni Perri
resignes from this position.

The precision disturbed him more than the content. Not "might resign" or "could
resign." Not a probability distribution. A fixed point. As if the machine itself
were the attractor drawing the future toward it.

He tried variations. Different starting conditions, different time horizons. No
matter what he adjusted, the resignation remained. Fixed. Inevitable. The only
variable that changed was whether he learned this fact before or after 14:47.

At 14:20, his deputy knocked. Sara. Smart. Ambitious. She'd been waiting for him
to fail.

"The board is asking for results," she said. "They want to know if the machine
works."

Perri looked at her. He could simulate her career: she was in this room because
a hiring manager's wife suggested her name at dinner. She'd survived three
layoffs because a VP's daughter went to school with her. She'd rise higher than
he ever did, not because she was brilliant, but because she had the right
accidents.

"It works," he said.

"And? What does it show?"

"That success is luck. All of it. Merit is real, but it's never the decisive
factor."

Sara smiled. "Then we keep this quiet. Use it to predict markets, select hires.
Imagine the advantage—"

"That's not what I'm going to do."

At 14:43, Perri stood at the machine's primary console. Four minutes until the
simulation's predicted moment. He felt something rising in his chest—a
conviction about destiny, about character, about the choices that matter.

At 14:44, he understood.

The conviction itself was in the simulation. The machine had shown him the
future—_himself having this conviction_—and the conviction was real and it was
determined and it was exactly what he needed to make the choice the machine
predicted. The machine had not trapped him. The machine had freed him into the
precise choice he was meant to make.

He turned the machine off at 14:46.

Not to save the world—the last simulation showed humanity continuing perfectly
well without ever knowing the truth. People would prosper, fail, thrive, and
suffer, all while believing in their own agency. They would be happy.

But he had seen the simulation that showed them _knowing_ the truth. In those
branches, people sat in corridors waiting for luck that, once seen, refused to
happen. Knowledge created a paralysis that luck itself could not break.

Because a world governed by luck was intolerable. But a world that _knew_ it was
governed by luck could not function at all.

The screen went dark. The humming stopped.

Above him, the river continued its indifferent flow, muffling nothing, deciding
everything.
