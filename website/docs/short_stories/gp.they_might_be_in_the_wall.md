---
title: They Might Be in the Wall
---

Right before falling asleep, I heard a sound in the wall. Not the ordinary creak
of an old house settling, but something deliberate. Something alive. Scratching,
then silence, then again, in the corner on my wife's side.

My wife stirred beside me. "Do you hear that?"

"Yes."

"They're in the wall," she said flatly. "The rats. They can get out, you know.
They can chew through. What if they come through while we're sleeping? They can
eat our noses, I heard"

I said nothing. I had heard it too, and I had already thought the same thing,
without the dramatic ending.

Silence again for a few minutes. Then sound again, something gnawing, something
using its little paws to scratch against the inside of the wall. An insistent
sound of something trying to escape, as if its life depended on it.

Silence again. Same sound in a slightly different place, a bit closer to the
headboard.

My wife complained about the sounds, uneasy. I promised to call the exterminator
first thing in the morning. This must be a common problem for houses in the
countryside, full of animal life, insects, bugs, rodents.

We held our breath for five more minutes without any sound, then we sighed and
both fell asleep. I slept well, despite the potential threat to our noses.

The next night there was no sound at all, nothing for several days.

I thought it was just a fluke, maybe the little ones were outside the external
wall (nothing to do then, the outside part of the house belongs to them). I was
happy that nothing needed to be added to my already overflowing to-do list and
probably some money saved. Things around the house are always more expensive
than what one expects, and there is a surprising phenomenon that when you look
to solve a problem, you find many more that need solving. Probably some nest of
snakes in the deep countryside, scratching against the plaster, coming and going
with the seasons.

The countryside on our part of the country crawls with life.

A few days later, again the same insistent scratch.

I wasn't worried personally about my nose being bitten off. I was more annoyed
by the extra work needed to get rid of the animals before they started chewing
on wires, which would surely require expensive fixes. I was also annoyed by the
timing of these creatures, which mainly worried my wife, which made me worried
in turn. They always scratched on days when I had to wake up early to drive to
the university.

A couple of days after, I called the exterminator.

He came on a Tuesday afternoon and walked outside the house with the methodical
air of someone performing a familiar duty. He looked for holes. He spoke of
poisons and traps, of checking the walls, of ensuring that if the rats died
inside the cavities, we would know where to find them. For property value, he
said. Dead rodents rot quickly and the smell spreads.

"Rodents are the only thing that survived the great extinction of dinosaurs," I
said to him, remembering something from a podcast I had heard during my weekly
commute. "In a certain sense we owe our existence to them." The exterminator
nodded without interest at my awkward remark and left me an invoice. He probably
wanted to get the work done quickly so he could spend the rest of his the
allocated appointment window browsing photos on his phone.

The nights that followed were quiet. Then the scratching returned, softer now,
as if something had learned patience.

Every Wednesday morning, I speak to my mother at seven in the morning. I do this
while driving to the university, a routine I have maintained for years. She
pretends she is busy when I call, but I know she has been waiting for my call.
The conversation follows a familiar protocol.

First, we discuss the weather. How it is getting crazier. How spring is almost
here, or summer is coming, or the wind is causing her problems with the
cervicale. Cervicale is a Southern Italian non-real medical condition, a
stiffness of the neck to be precise, believed to be caused drafts of air (from a
car window, when sleeping near a fan, or by a pesky gust through a window) or
improper hair drying.

Then we talk about my sister. How she is doing. How her daughters are doing in
school, growing up, becoming young women. These are the middle passages, the
expected transitions.

Then we discuss my children. Their schools. Their small achievements. She nods
along through the telephone line, though I cannot see her nodding. She complains
that I have sent her the same pictures of the kids multiple times, and I need to
take more photos and be more careful about repetition. I agree to make her happy
that she still has control over me, like if I was her baby.

Then we discuss what she has eaten or what she is planning to eat, the marvel of
the air fryer, a new thing in Italy that finally combines the taste of fried
food with the healthiness of a normal oven.

And finally, always finally, comes the grand finale. The recitation of the dead.
Who died this week. Who had an accident.

"Do you remember Tito?" she asked one Wednesday.

"I'm not sure," I said. Sometimes I did remember people from childhood with
their names appearing suddenly.

"The carpenter. He was blonde. He was in your elementary school. A wall fell on
him last Wednesday."

"Oh yes, I remember him now. Did he fall from a wall?"

"No no, a wall fell on him. They were repairing something on top of a building.
The wall was old, and while they were working, it fell on him and crushed him.
He was a good man. Too young."

On another Wednesday: "The dentist. The one who had the computer you liked. He
had a stroke playing padel with his son. He's in a coma now. They don't think
he'll wake."

The computer my mother was referring to was an Apple Macintosh. I remember it
perfectly. It sat in his office, gray and beautiful, like a Michelangelo's
sculpture. The dentist would show me the screen, the mouse, the icons. I was
absolutely mesmerized. I can still recall the smell of it, that new plastic
scent. I asked my parents what I would have to do to have a computer like that
for Christmas. I was already a good student, obedient, well-behaved. Surely I
could earn it. My parents would just say computers were useless and I didn't
really need one. What would I do with it? Play video games and become stupido?
It was easy for them to find excuses not to buy things when we couldn't afford
them. And that was often. I still think of that gray computer. I could have
achieved everything if I had only possessed it.

A scratching in the wall. A sea of rodents. Rodents walking on rodents, hissing
at other rodents.

And another terrible story: "Do you remember that girl? Tall, dark hair, blue
eyes, kind of pretty. They lived down the street in Via San Cesareo, in that
broken down house that used to be a horse stall. You played volleyball with her
little brother in high school."

"Vaguely, Mamma. That was thirty years ago," I said. But I did remember her
well, and she was indeed very pretty. She would walk through the street alone,
wrapped in heavy clothes during winter, while I watched people from the window
of my room.

"She killed herself. Last month. No one understood why."

Another anxious scratching inside the wall. The rodents frantically trying to
escape. Rodents biting other rodents.

"We should sell," my wife said finally in the silence.

"And tell the buyers what? That the walls are haunted by rats?"

"Yes," she said.

Another Wednesday: "Guess what happened to Nicola?"

"Nicola? Who?"

"The peasant who lived near us. The one who went to jail for drugs, although I
am sure he was set up. They all knew he was framed. He finally came out of jail.
No one would speak to him. His wife had left. His children never visited. One
day he was cutting an olive tree and fell from the tree. He hit his head on a
rock. Dead immediately. There was nothing anyone could do."

Each story landed with the weight of casual fact. My mother presented each death
as if it were weather, something that simply occurred, neither surprising nor
preventable. And each time she told me of someone I had known, someone from a
world I had left behind nearly 30 years before moving to Napoli for college and
then to America. I would recall their faces, some small details, and now they
were gone after living a life of many details I didn't know about. Falling in
love, getting married, having children, pain, and disease.

It would begin small, almost apologetic, then grow. Louder. More insistent. More
numerous, as if something were accumulating in the dark spaces behind the
plaster, gathering, building its army.

I asked my wife if she noticed it. She looked at me strangely.

Even if my job didn't revolve around finding patterns, it was easy to notice the
scratching always around the days I would have to wake up early to drive to
college and talk to my mother.

"It's always after I talk to my mother," I said. "The scratching gets louder.
For an hour or two afterward, it's worse."

What if the wall was not simply harboring rodents? Each death that entered my
ear through the telephone entered the walls as well — names being recorded,
catalogued, added to the wall's inventory of the gone.

The exterminator returned with boxes of poison. He set traps and sealed holes.
For a few days the scratching continued, sometimes soft, sometimes violent,
sometimes directly beneath my palm when I pressed it against the plaster in the
dark.

The exterminator would come again to check the traps. The wall would scratch
softly, patiently, waiting for the Wednesday mornings and telephone calls,
waiting for each new name to be added to its record.

I still speak to my mother every Wednesday. I still drive to work while she
tells me who has died, who has fallen, who cheats on his wife, who has given up.
And I still hear the wall scratching in response, recording, remembering,
keeping count.

Sometimes I wonder if she can hear it too, on her end of the line. That distant
sound in the background. The sound of the wall keeping score.

Sometimes I wonder if all walls do this. If every house in every town contains a
wall like mine, patient, scratching down the names of everyone we have ever
known who has stopped being alive. If that is the case, we should not be afraid.
